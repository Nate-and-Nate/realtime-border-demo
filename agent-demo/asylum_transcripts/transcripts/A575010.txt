ASYLUM INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT
Case ID: A575010
Case Type: Affirmative Asylum
Date of Interview: 2024-04-19

INTERVIEW BEGINS

OFFICER: Good morning, Ms. Elena Rodriguez. My name is Officer Miller, and this is Officer Davis. We are Asylum Officers with U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services. This interview is to determine if you are eligible for asylum in the United States. You have applied for asylum, and we will now ask you questions about your application and your reasons for fearing return to Guatemala. Do you understand?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Si, entiendo. Yes, I understand.

OFFICER: I need to swear you in before we begin. Please raise your right hand. Do you swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth, so help you God?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Si, juro. Yes, I swear.

OFFICER: Thank you. Please be seated. Let the record reflect that the applicant has been sworn in. Ms. Rodriguez, you are represented by counsel, is that correct?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Si, es correcto. Yes, that is correct.

OFFICER: Please state your name for the record.

ATTORNEY: Maria Hernandez, representing Ms. Rodriguez.

OFFICER: Thank you, Ms. Hernandez. Ms. Rodriguez, can you please state your name and date of birth for the record?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Mi nombre es Elena Maria Rodriguez. My name is Elena Maria Rodriguez. I born… (struggles to find the English word) … on… April 10th, 1996.

OFFICER: Thank you. Ms. Rodriguez, please confirm your address as it appears on your application, [reads address]. Is that correct?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Si, correcto. Yes, correct.

OFFICER: Okay. Let's start with some background information. Where in Guatemala were you born and raised?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Nací y crecí en Chimaltenango. I was born and raised in Chimaltenango. It’s… (struggles again) … a town near Guatemala City.

OFFICER: And what is the highest level of education you completed?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Terminé la escuela secundaria. I finished secondary school. High school.

OFFICER: Did you receive a diploma or certificate upon completion?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Si, recibí un diploma. Yes, I received a diploma. I can… I have copy.

OFFICER: Okay, we have a copy in your file. Now, what kind of work did you do in Guatemala?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Trabajé en construcción. I worked in construction. I learned from my father.

OFFICER: Can you describe the type of construction work you did?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Ayudaba a construir casas. I helped build houses. Mixing cement, carrying bricks… sometimes, the… (struggles) … the roof.

OFFICER: Did you have any specific skills or training in construction?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Aprendí en el trabajo. I learned on the job. My father taught me how to read blueprints, how to measure, how to… (struggles) … to make walls strong.

OFFICER: Okay. Now, Ms. Rodriguez, let's talk about why you left Guatemala. Your application states you fear persecution due to your political opinion. Can you tell me more about that?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Si. It started… (hesitates) … with the elections. The… the mayor.

OFFICER: Which elections are you referring to?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) The last one. Two years ago. In my town.

OFFICER: So, the mayoral elections in Chimaltenango approximately two years ago. What happened then?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) My family… we supported the other candidate. The one who promised to… (struggles) … to help the poor people.

OFFICER: And what did you do to support this candidate?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) We talked to our neighbors. We put up posters. We… we went to the meetings. Small meetings.

OFFICER: Did you publicly express your support for this candidate?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Si. I wore a… a shirt with his name. I spoke at one meeting. Not a big speech. Just… just saying he was a good man.

OFFICER: And what was the reaction to your support for this candidate?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) At first… nothing. But after… after the other candidate won… the one we didn't like… things changed.

OFFICER: How did things change?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) His… his people… the people who supported him… they started… (hesitates) … they started watching us.

OFFICER: Who are these "people" you are referring to? Were they police, government officials, or something else?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No… not police. They were… men. Men who worked for him. Some wore… wore guns. They were… (struggles) … intimidating.

OFFICER: Can you describe any specific incidents where you felt threatened or harassed?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. One night… maybe two months after the election… I was walking home from my friend’s house. It was dark. Two men stopped me. They said… (hesitates) … they said I was a… a traitor. They said I should be careful what I say.

OFFICER: Did they physically harm you?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No… but they grabbed my arm. Hard. They pushed me. They said… they knew where my family lived.

OFFICER: Did you report this incident to the police?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No. The police… they are friends with the mayor. They wouldn't help.

OFFICER: Why do you believe the police are friends with the mayor?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Everyone knows. He pays them. They protect him. If I went to the police, they would tell him. It would be worse.

OFFICER: Okay. What happened after that incident?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) It got worse. They started… (struggles) … vandalizing our house. Throwing rocks at the windows. Painting… bad words on the wall.

OFFICER: What kind of bad words?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) (Through interpreter, whispers to the interpreter) Words like… "traitor," "communist," "die."

OFFICER: Did you see who was doing this?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Not always. But one time… I saw one of the men who stopped me on the street. He was watching our house.

OFFICER: Can you describe this man?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) He was… tall. Maybe six feet. Dark hair. He had a… a scar on his face. Near his eye.

OFFICER: Did you recognize him from anywhere else besides the incident on the street?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No. Just that one time.

OFFICER: Did these incidents continue?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. For many months. Then… then they attacked my father.

OFFICER: When did this attack happen?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) About six months ago. I remember it was… (thinks) … it was a Tuesday.

OFFICER: Can you describe what happened?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) My father… he was coming home from work. He was walking on the street. Two men… the same men, I think… they jumped him. They beat him. They broke his arm.

OFFICER: Did you see the attack?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No. But my neighbor saw. She told me.

OFFICER: Did you report this attack to the police?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No. Same reason. The police… they wouldn't help.

OFFICER: What happened after your father was attacked?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) We took him to the hospital. He was… he was very hurt. After that… we knew we had to leave.

OFFICER: Why did you decide to leave Guatemala instead of moving to another part of the country?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) The mayor… he has power everywhere. He has friends everywhere. We couldn't go anywhere safe. And… my uncle, he lives here, in Los Angeles. He is a citizen. He said he would help me.

OFFICER: So, you have family ties to citizens here in the United States?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. My uncle, my father's brother. He is a good man.

OFFICER: Ms. Hernandez, do you have anything to add regarding Ms. Rodriguez's family ties in the U.S.?

ATTORNEY: Yes, Officer. We have submitted documentation confirming Ms. Rodriguez's uncle's citizenship and a declaration from him stating his willingness to provide support to Ms. Rodriguez.

OFFICER: Thank you. Ms. Rodriguez, how did you travel to the United States?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I… I paid someone. A… a coyote. I traveled with other people. It was… it was very hard.

OFFICER: Can you describe the route you took?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) We went through Mexico. We walked a lot. We rode in trucks. It took… maybe three weeks.

OFFICER: Where did you cross the border into the United States?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Near Tijuana.

OFFICER: And when did you enter the United States?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) (Thinks) Two months ago. February.

OFFICER: You entered the United States approximately two months ago near Tijuana. You then filed your application for asylum. Correct?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Si, correcto. Yes, correct.

OFFICER: Ms. Rodriguez, do you have any documents or evidence to support your claims of persecution?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I have… photos of the graffiti on my house. And… a copy of my father’s hospital bill.

OFFICER: Are these documents included with your application?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Si. Yes.

OFFICER: Okay, we have those. Ms. Rodriguez, if you are granted asylum, what are your plans for the future?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I want to work. I want to… to build things. I want to help my family. I want to be safe.

OFFICER: Given your background in construction, do you plan to pursue work in that field here in the United States?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Si. I am a hard worker. I can learn new skills. I want to… to contribute.

OFFICER: Do you speak any English?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) A little. I am learning.

OFFICER: Ms. Rodriguez, is there anything else you would like to add or any other information you think is important for us to know?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I just… I just want to be safe. I can’t go back. They will hurt me. They will hurt my family.

OFFICER: Ms. Hernandez, do you have any final comments or questions for your client?

ATTORNEY: Yes, Officer. I would like to reiterate that Ms. Rodriguez has presented credible and consistent testimony regarding the persecution she and her family suffered in Guatemala due to their political opinion. The evidence provided, including the photos and medical records, corroborates her claims. Furthermore, her uncle's willingness to provide support demonstrates her potential for successful integration into American society.

OFFICER: Thank you, Ms. Hernandez. Ms. Rodriguez, we have no further questions for you at this time. We will review your application and your testimony, and you will receive a decision in the mail. Do you have any questions for us?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No. Thank you.

OFFICER: Thank you for your time, Ms. Rodriguez.

INTERVIEW ENDS
