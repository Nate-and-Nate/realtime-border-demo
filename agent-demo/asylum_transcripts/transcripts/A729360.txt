ASYLUM INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT
Case ID: A729360

Date of Interview: 2023-02-14

INTERVIEW BEGINS

OFFICER: Good morning, Mr. Khan. My name is Officer Miller, and this is Officer Davis. We are asylum officers with U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services. This interview is to determine if you are eligible for asylum in the United States. I need to inform you of your rights and responsibilities during this interview. You have the right to an attorney; however, I see that you are not represented today. Is that correct?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes, correct. No lawyer.

OFFICER: Okay. You have the right to present evidence on your behalf and to testify under oath. You must answer all questions truthfully. If you knowingly provide false information, you could face serious consequences, including criminal prosecution and removal from the United States. Do you understand these rights and responsibilities?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes, I understand.

OFFICER: Do you have any questions before we begin?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No, I think… I think I understand.

OFFICER: Okay. Please raise your right hand. Do you swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth, so help you God?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I swear.

OFFICER: Thank you. Please be seated. Let’s begin by confirming some basic information. Your name is Omar Khan, and your date of birth is January 10, 1976. Is that correct?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes, that is correct.

OFFICER: And you are a citizen of Afghanistan?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes, I am Afghan.

OFFICER: You are currently residing in [Applicant's Address in the US]?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes, in [Applicant's Address in the US].

OFFICER: Alright, Mr. Khan, you are seeking asylum in the United States based on a fear of persecution in Afghanistan. Can you tell me why you are afraid to return to Afghanistan? Please speak slowly and clearly, and we will use the interpreter to ensure everything is accurately recorded.

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Okay. I am… I am afraid because I am… I am Pashtun. And because of my family… my family has… had problems with… with other groups.

OFFICER: You said you are Pashtun. Can you elaborate on the problems your family faced because of your ethnicity?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. In my province, [Province Name], many different groups. Tajik, Hazara, Uzbek… and Pashtun. Always… always problems. My family… my father… he was a businessman. He… he owned a small shop… selling… selling clothes and… and some food.

OFFICER: What kind of problems did your father experience?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Other groups… they… they did not like Pashtuns. They… they said we were… we were supporters of the Taliban. This is not true. My father… he was a good man. He just wanted to… to make a living. But they… they harassed him.

OFFICER: Can you give me specific examples of this harassment?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. Many times… they would come to his shop and… and demand money. They said it was… it was for… for their group. Protection money. If he did not pay… they would… they would threaten him. They… they broke his windows once. Another time… they… they stole some of his goods.

OFFICER: Can you give me dates or approximate dates for when these incidents occurred?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) This… this was happening for… for many years. From… maybe 2010… until… until I left. The window… the window broken… that was in… maybe 2015. The stolen goods… that was in 2017.

OFFICER: Did your father report these incidents to the police?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) (Hesitates) No. No, he did not.

OFFICER: Why not?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) The police… they were… they were corrupt. They… they would not help. And sometimes… they were… they were also involved. They would… they would take money from my father too.

OFFICER: So, your father faced harassment and extortion due to his ethnicity. Did this harassment escalate in any way?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. In… in 2019… things got worse. My father… he was… he was attacked.

OFFICER: Attacked? Can you tell me more about this attack?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) (Becoming emotional) Yes. He was… he was walking home from the shop. It was… it was late. Two men… they… they stopped him. They… they beat him. They… they called him names… Pashtun names. They… they said he was a Taliban supporter. They… they took his money.

OFFICER: How badly was he injured?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) He… he was… he was badly hurt. He… he had… he had broken ribs. And… and a broken arm. He… he was in the hospital for… for several days.

OFFICER: Did you report this attack to the police?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No. We… we were too afraid. We… we knew they would not help. And we were afraid that… that the attackers would come back.

OFFICER: After this attack, what did your family do?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) We… we tried to… to be more careful. My father… he stopped going to the shop alone. I… I would go with him. But… but we were still afraid.

OFFICER: Did the harassment continue after the attack?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. It… it was not as… as violent. But they… they would still shout at us. They would… they would make threats. They would… they would say that we should leave. That we did not belong there.

OFFICER: Why do you believe you were targeted specifically because you are Pashtun?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Because… because they said so. They… they called us Pashtun names. They… they blamed us for… for the Taliban. They… they said that all Pashtuns are… are bad people.

OFFICER: Did you ever consider moving to a different part of Afghanistan where you might be safer?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. We… we thought about it. But… but we did not have the money. And… and we did not know where to go. We… we heard that… that other Pashtuns were also having problems in… in other areas.

OFFICER: What is your educational background, Mr. Khan?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I… I finished secondary school. High school.

OFFICER: And after high school, did you pursue any further education or training?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No. I… I started working with my father in his shop.

OFFICER: Can you describe your role in your father's business?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I… I helped him with… with everything. Buying goods… selling goods… managing the money. I… I learned a lot about… about business.

OFFICER: So, you have experience in managing a small business. What specific skills do you possess that you think would be useful here in the United States?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I… I am good with… with money. I… I am good at… at talking to people. I… I am hard working. I… I am willing to learn.

OFFICER: What were the circumstances that led you to leave Afghanistan?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) After… after the Taliban… after the Taliban took over… things became much worse.

OFFICER: Can you describe how the Taliban's takeover affected you and your family?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) They… they came to our neighborhood. They… they started enforcing… their rules. Very strict rules. They… they said that… that men must grow beards. That women must cover their faces. They… they stopped girls from going to school.

OFFICER: How did the Taliban's presence affect your family's safety and well-being specifically?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) They… they started asking questions about… about my father. About his business. About… about our family. They… they said that we were… we were not… good Muslims. That we were… we were too Westernized.

OFFICER: Did the Taliban directly threaten you or your family?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) They… they did not… directly threaten us. But… but we were afraid. We… we saw what they were doing to other people. We… we heard stories about… about people being punished for… for small things. We… we knew that it was only a matter of time before… before they came for us.

OFFICER: What happened to your father's business after the Taliban took over?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) It… it became very difficult. People… people did not have money to spend. And the Taliban… they… they were taking money from businesses. My father… he… he had to close the shop.

OFFICER: When did you leave Afghanistan?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I left in… in November… 2021.

OFFICER: Can you describe your journey from Afghanistan to the United States?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) It was… it was very difficult. I… I went to… to Pakistan first. Then… then to Turkey. Then… then to Mexico. And then… then I crossed the border into the United States.

OFFICER: How did you enter the United States?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I… I crossed the border… near… near [Border City].

OFFICER: Did you have any assistance from smugglers or other individuals along the way?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. I… I paid someone to… to help me.

OFFICER: Can you provide more details about the individuals who assisted you and the amounts you paid?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I… I paid different people at different times. I… I don't remember all the names. I… I paid them to… to help me cross borders. To… to find transportation.

OFFICER: Do you have any documents with you today that you would like to submit as evidence in support of your asylum claim?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I… I have some… some photos of… of my father's shop. And… and a copy of his… his business license. I… I also have a… a medical report from when he was… he was attacked.

OFFICER: Okay, please provide those documents to us. We will review them. Now, you mentioned your family. Who is still in Afghanistan?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) My… my wife. She is still there.

OFFICER: Is she in danger?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. I… I am very worried about her. She is… she is Pashtun too. And she is… she is alone. She… she cannot work. She… she cannot go out without a… a male relative. She… she is very scared.

OFFICER: Are you planning to bring her to the United States if you are granted asylum?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Yes. That is… that is my biggest wish. To… to bring her here… to safety.

OFFICER: Mr. Khan, you have stated that your father’s business was targeted. Do you have any documentation besides the business license that proves that your father owned the business? For example, tax records?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) No, I do not have tax records. My father kept most things in cash. The business was small.

OFFICER: You mentioned you have a medical report from when your father was attacked. Is this an official medical report from a hospital?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) (Hesitates) It is… it is from a doctor. But… but it is not… not from a big hospital. From a… a small clinic.

OFFICER: Do you have any other relatives in Afghanistan who are also at risk?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I have… I have some cousins. But… but I am not as worried about them. They are… they are not as… visible as my father was.

OFFICER: If you are granted asylum in the United States, what are your plans for the future?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I want to… to work. To… to support myself. To… to learn English. To… to bring my wife here. I… I want to… to start a new life. I… I want to… to be safe. Maybe… maybe I can find work in a shop. Or… or maybe I can… I can learn a new skill.

OFFICER: What are your current means of support here in the United States?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) I… I am staying with… with a friend. He… he is helping me. I… I am looking for work.

OFFICER: Do you have any questions for us before we conclude this interview?

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) (Hesitates) What… what happens now?

OFFICER: We will review all the information you have provided, including your testimony and the documents you submitted. We will then make a decision on your case. You will receive a written notice in the mail informing you of our decision. It could take several months.

APPLICANT: (Through interpreter) Okay. Thank you.

OFFICER: Thank you, Mr. Khan. That concludes our interview.

INTERVIEW ENDS
